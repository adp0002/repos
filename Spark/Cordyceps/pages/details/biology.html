Biology of Cordyceps militaris

Overview

Cordyceps militaris is an entomopathogenic fungus belonging to the family Cordycipitaceae. It parasitizes insects—particularly lepidopteran larvae—and produces characteristic orange club-shaped fruiting bodies. It is valued for bioactive compounds such as cordycepin, adenosine, polysaccharides, and carotenoids.

Cellular Biology

  • Hyphae: Septate, filamentous hyphae capable of penetrating insect cuticle.
  • Reproduction: Sexual reproduction via perithecia and ascospores; asexual reproduction via conidia.
  • Spore Germination: Requires nutrient-rich, moist conditions; produces germ tubes that invade hosts or substrates.
  • Metabolism: Facultative aerobic metabolism with enzymatic machinery to degrade insect tissues and plant-based substrates.

How C. militaris Utilizes Nutrition

  • Carbohydrate Metabolism: Converts glucose and other sugars via glycolysis and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle to produce ATP. Excess glucose may be stored as fungal polysaccharides.
  • Protein & Amino Acid Use: Produces proteases to break down insect proteins into amino acids, which are reused for fungal protein synthesis and secondary metabolites such as cordycepin.
  • Lipid Utilization: Extracts fatty acids and sterols from insect hemolymph; incorporates them into membranes and signaling molecules critical for sporulation.
  • Mineral Uptake: Regulated transporters uptake trace minerals; iron and copper are critical for redox enzymes; zinc supports DNA-binding proteins.
  • Polysaccharide Production: Synthesizes extracellular polysaccharides (EPS) for structural functions and bioactive properties, requiring balanced carbon-to-nitrogen ratio.

Biochemical Pathways Relevant to Nutrition

  • Glycolysis → TCA cycle: Main energy generation route.
  • Pentose phosphate pathway: Provides NADPH and ribose-5-phosphate for biosynthesis.
  • Secondary metabolism: Cordycepin and adenosine synthesis pathways depend on nitrogen and energy balance.
  • Enzymatic secretion: Chitinases, proteases, and lipases degrade insect cuticle and tissues to release nutrients.

Environmental and Physiological Factors

  • Optimal temperature: 18–22 °C for fruiting, 22–24 °C for mycelial growth.
  • Relative humidity: 70–90% depending on stage.
  • Light cycles: 12–16 hours light stimulates pigmentation and fruiting body development.
  • pH tolerance: Optimal between 5.5–6.5.

Host Insect Mimicry in Synthetic Substrate

C. militaris naturally derives chitin, proteins, lipids, and minerals from insects. Synthetic substrates aim to mimic this composition using plant-derived starches, proteins (soy, casein), lipids (vegetable oils), and mineral salts. Adjusting C:N ratio (~15–20:1) is essential for balancing energy production with secondary metabolite synthesis.
