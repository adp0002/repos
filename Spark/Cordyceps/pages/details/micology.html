🧬 Taxonomy & Identity

  • Kingdom Fungi • Phylum Ascomycota • Class Sordariomycetes • Order Hypocreales • Genus Cordyceps
  • Entomopathogenic fungus targeting lepidopteran larvae and pupae in nature.
  • Key trait: production of cordycepin (3′-deoxyadenosine) and adenosine.
Tip: Maintain master cultures on low‑nutrient slants (e.g., MEA) at 4 °C; subculture every 2–3 months to minimize senescence.

🧫 Life Cycle & Morphology

  1. Spore (conidia) germination on nutrient surface (PDA/MEA).
  2. Hyphal growth → mycelium with septate hyphae; orange pigmentation common.
  3. Primordia (hyphal knots) form under light & aeration triggers.
  4. Fruiting bodies (club‑shaped stromata) develop and accumulate metabolites.
Germination window: 18–22 °C, moderate moisture, low light.
Fruiting trigger: light exposure, lower CO₂, high RH, stable temps.

Goal: provide balanced C:N, trace minerals (Mg, Zn, Fe, Se), lipids/sterols, and chitin to mimic insect hosts.

💡 Environment Control

Incubation (mycelial)

  • 20–22 °C • RH 60–70% • darkness • 10–15 days
  • High CO₂ tolerated; minimal handling.

Fruiting

  • 20–22 °C • RH 80–90% • CO₂ < 1000 ppm
  • Light 500–1000 lux, 12–14 h/day (green 520–540 nm or red:blue 1:1).
Air exchange: 2–4 brief exchanges/day or filtered fans. Avoid direct drafts that desiccate primordia.

📋 Cultivation SOP (Jar Method)

  1. Prepare master culture: Maintain on MEA/PDA at 20–22 °C.
  2. Liquid spawn: 100 mL PDB with agar wedge; shake 120–150 RPM for 5–7 days.
  3. Substrate load: 100–120 g per 1 L jar (55–60% moisture).
  4. Sterilize: 121 °C, 60–90 min; cool overnight.
  5. Inoculate: 5–10 mL liquid spawn or 1–2 agar wedges; breathable lids.
  6. Incubate: 10–15 days until full colonization.
  7. Induce fruiting: Move to lighted, humid chamber per specs above.
  8. Harvest: 30–45 days post‑inoculation when stromata are bright orange and firm.
  9. Dry: 40–50 °C to <6% moisture; store airtight, dark, cool.
QC: log temp/RH/light daily; photo‑document growth; sample for cordycepin by HPLC if available.

🧪 Biochemistry & Metabolites

  • Cordycepin (3′‑deoxyadenosine): RNA chain terminator; peaks during fruiting.
  • Adenosine, D‑mannitol, ergosterol, polysaccharides.
  • Key genes: cns1–cns4 cluster; transport via ABC transporter (cns4).
  • Up‑regulation under light stress, moderate hypoxia, and balanced C:N.
Boosting strategies
  • Trace Se/Zn inclusion; green or red‑blue LED exposure (12–14 h/day).
  • Static/liquid two‑stage fermentation for higher cordycepin in broth.
  • Maintain CO₂ under 1000 ppm during fruiting; avoid temperature swings.

📈 Analytics & QA/QC

  • HPLC/LC‑MS: cordycepin & adenosine quantification.
  • Microscopy: hyphal health, spore morphology.
  • Moisture assay: gravimetric to verify ≤6% in dried product.
  • Environmental logs: temp/RH/CO₂/light charting per batch.

📚 Glossary

  • Conidia: Asexual spores produced by C. militaris.
  • Stroma: Club‑like fruiting body bearing spores.
  • Primordia: Initial fruiting structures arising from mycelium.
  • C:N Ratio: Carbon to nitrogen balance in substrate.
  • Senescence: Loss of vigor after many subcultures.

❓ FAQ

What light spectrum works best?

Green (520–540 nm) or a balanced red:blue mix at 500–1000 lux, 12–14 h/day. Blue aids primordia; red supports morphogenesis. Avoid overheating LEDs.

Can I reuse substrate for a second flush?

Yes, if contamination‑free. Expect reduced yield; re‑humidify environment and keep CO₂ low.

Rice vs insect‑mimic substrates?

Rice is simple and reliable. Insect‑mimic blends (protein, chitin, minerals) can enhance bioactives but require strict moisture control.
